Yes, Minnesotans, February was as bad as you thought.

After an unusually slow start, winter decided make up for lost time — dumping 37 inches of snow in 28 days and essentially transforming the Twin Cities into a mountain town without the mountains.

Literally.

Our snowfall totals had more in common with Jackson, Wyo., (41 inches) than our fellow Midwesterners in Chicago (9 inches), Milwaukee (18 inches) and Detroit (5.5 inches). Our 37 inches put us level with the summit of New Hampshire's Mt. Washington — elevation 6,288 feet — a place that proudly claims to be home to the worst weather in America.

In fact, with the exception of Wisconsin and Michigan's Upper Peninsula, the Twin Cities endured more snow this February than nearly everywhere outside the Mountain West, according to national snowfall estimates.

This, to put it lightly, is unusual.

Despite our reputation for brutal winters, the Twin Cities averages about 10 inches of snow in February — comparable to much of New England, the upper Midwest, and swaths of the northern plains. This year we put them all to shame.

The maps below show how our snowfall compares to other regions, both this year and over an average of the previous five. Red areas show spots that got at least as much snow as the Twin Cities. Blue areas got less.

And if misery loves company, the maps also show how lonely our February truly was.

New England is no stranger to snowy winters. Apart from being home to several major mountain ranges, the region is often battered by ferocious nor'easters that churn up the coast and dump snow by the foot in areas like Boston and New York.

A particularly brutal series of storms in 2015 dropped nearly six feet on Boston in Feburary alone, on the way to a record-setting season of 108 inches — a total the Twin Cities has never matched.

This year, Boston got 8 inches. Portland, Maine, managed nearly 11. And up north, Burlington, Vt., tallied 16.

In most winters, we can usually count on our Midwestern compatriots in Milwaukee, Chicago and Detroit to share our pain.

Each averages a similar amount of February snowfall, made worse at times by frigid winds ripping off the Great Lakes.

It could be worse.

Areas of the Sierra Nevada, in California, have already been socked with more than 300 inches of snow in February.

That's good news for Los Angeles, which counts on snowmelt from the Sierra for its water supply, but it has also shut down highways, triggered enormous avalanches, and created memeworthy snowdrifts the size of small buildings.
